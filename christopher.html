Overview

Overview of my final paper

A summary of my contributions and key findings in the LAIDS Project:

My contribution to the LAIDS Project involved developing and evaluating two lightweight AI-based Intrusion Detection Systems optimised for resource-constrained networks. These models were designed to address security vulnerabilities in low-resource environments such as IoT and edge devices while maintaining strong detection capabilities.

  • A hybrid Convolutional Neural Network / Gated Recurrent Unit (CNN-GRU) model
  • A hybrid Generative Adversarial Network / Multilayer Perceptron (GAN-MLP) model

These models were benchmarked against a baseline 1D CNN-based IDS using the CICIDS 2017 dataset. To further investigate model efficiency, I restructured the baseline into a 2D CNN to analyse how an added spatial dimension impacts detection accuracy and resource usage.

Results showed that both the 2D CNN and CNN-GRU models performed well for lightweight deployment. The CNN-GRU achieved the best overall multi-class classification accuracy, while the 2D CNN excelled at detecting unseen attacks. However, the GAN-MLP model underperformed due to architectural limitations inherent to lightweight adversarial frameworks.

Research Question

Problem Statement & Research Question

The LAIDS project addresses the vulnerability of low-resource environments such as IoT networks and edge devices to cyberattacks. Existing IDSs are either too resource-intensive or lack the ability to detect zero-day attacks. I chose to explore the application of a GAN-MLP and CNN-GRU based IDS. As such my paper sought to answer the question:

How can a Hybrid CNN-GRU or GAN-based IDS outperform a CNN-based IDS in terms of zero-day attack detection, detection accuracy, false positive rate, and computational resource usage in a resource-constrained network environment?

Experiment Overview

Experiment 1: Multi Classification Performance

This experiment evaluates the multi-classification performance of proposed models and establishes a baseline benchmark for each of the architectures. The models were trained with preprocessing, early stopping, and validation monitoring, while GANs required manual selection. The models were evaluated on their: accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score and false positive rate.

Experiment 2: Post-Training Quantisation

This experiment assesses the impact of post-training quantization on model performance and resource use. The baseline models underwent dynamic and full quantization including FP32, FP16 and INT8 precisions. They were then evaluated on their multi-classification performance and computational efficiency. The models’ CPU utilization and resident set size were measured to quantify their resource requirements.

Experiment 3: Zero-Day Attack Detection

This experiment evaluates the models’ ability to generalise towards zero-day attacks. The baseline models were retrained with the DoS and DDoS attack types removed from training and validation sets. They were evaluated based on the amount of zero-day attacks they were able to flag.

Results

Literature Review & Final Paper

Literature Review

Summary:

My literature review summarises current academic research on AI-IDSs for resource-constrained networks. It compares traditional IDSs with AI-based methods by evaluating machine learning such as Support Vector Machines, Logistic Regression and K-means clustering for preprocessing and deep learning architectures such as CNNs, Autoencoders and GANs for improved detection. In this review I note that hybrid models strike a more effective balance between accuracy and efficiency, forming the foundation for the project’s CNN, CNN-GRU, and GAN-MLP implementations.

Final Paper

Summary:

My final paper outlines the LAIDS project’s complete methodology for developing and evaluating the proposed CNN-GRU and GAN-MLP models. It details the evaluation framework used to assess model performance across multiple criteria, including multi-classification ability, resource efficiency and ability to detect zero-day attacks. My paper also examines post-training quantization techniques and their impact on model size and computational performance, providing a comprehensive summary of the LAIDS project’s experimental design and implementation process for the 1D and 2D CNNs as well as the proposed CNN-GRU and GAN-MLP models.
