Overview of My Final Paper

A summary of my contributions and key findings in the LAIDS Study:

My contribution to the LAIDS Project involved developing, quantising, and evaluating three lightweight AI-based intrusion detection system models optimised for resource-constrained networks. The objective of this study was to investigate whether these models and their quantised variants could perform as well as or better than the baseline model and its quantised versions, while being more resource-efficient. Additionally, the study examined the models' ability to detect previously unseen attack classes in comparison to the baseline model.

These models developed were:

  • A baseline one-dimensional Convolutional Neural Network model (CNN), which was co-developed with my fellow LAIDS project members
  • A Principle Component Analysis enhanced Convolutional Neural Network model (PCA-CNN)
  • A hybrid Autoencoder-Multilayer Perceptron (AE-MLP) model

The models were trained and evaluated using the CICIDS2017 dataset across three experiments: benchmarking, quantisation, and generalisation to unseen data. Performance metrics such as accuracy, false negative rate, inference latency, and CPU time were recorded and compared to address the study's research questions.

The findings suggested that no model consistently outperformed the baseline in terms of detection performance and resource efficiency. However, each model demonstrated targeted performance gains over the baseline. Notably, the dynamically INT8-quantised PCA-CNN demonstrated the most promise for deployment as a lightweight intrusion detection system. The model achieved some of the lowest CPU processing times, inference latency, and false negative rates. Furthermore, the model attained the highest detection rate for DoS samples when they were given as zero-day attacks.

Literature Review

This literature review examines developments, challenges, and breakthroughs in intrusion detection system (IDS) frameworks. It specifically investigates the feasibility of implementing lightweight AI-based IDSs in low-resource networks. The findings of this review provided the foundation for the design and development of the lightweight AI-IDS models presented in the final study. This review examines both traditional and AI-based IDS methods, as well as existing datasets and commonly used evaluation metrics. It then provides a detailed comparison of traditional IDSs with AI-based IDSs, and of machine learning IDSs with deep learning IDSs. The feasibility of implementing a lightweight DL or ML-DL hybrid IDS is also assessed, along with the implications of the reviewed literature for the LAIDS project. Finally, conclusions are drawn, and a path forward for the LAIDS project is outlined.

Final Paper

Intrusion detection systems (IDSs) have become crucial to networks, particularly with the growth of IoT, rising cybercrime and increasingly sophisticated threats. Traditional intrusion detection systems struggle to scale effectively with these increases and to detect zero-day attacks. AI-based IDSs offer improved detection accuracy and adaptability, but are often too resource-intensive. The LAIDS (Lightweight AI-based Intrusion Detection System) project aims to address this gap by designing and evaluating lightweight AI-IDS models that balance strong detection capabilities with low resource usage. Three core model architectures are explored in this paper: a baseline CNN, a PCA-CNN and an AE-MLP, which are trained and evaluated with the CICIDS2017 dataset. These models, along with their quantised variants, are evaluated on their classification performance, resource efficiency and their ability to generalise to 'zero-day attacks'. The paper advances AI-based IDS solutions and demonstrates their suitability for resource-constrained environments.
