Overview of My Final Paper

A summary of my contributions and key findings in the LAIDS Study:

My contribution to the LAIDS Project involved developing, quantising, and evaluating three lightweight AI-based intrusion detection system models optimised for resource-constrained networks. The objective of this study was to investigate whether these models and their quantised variants could perform as well as or better than the baseline model and its quantised versions, while being more resource-efficient. Additionally, the study examined the models' ability to detect previously unseen attack classes in comparison to the baseline model.

These models developed were:

  • A baseline one-dimensional Convolutional Neural Network model (CNN), which was co-developed with my fellow LAIDS project members
  • A Principle Component Analysis enhanced Convolutional Neural Network model (PCA-CNN)
  • A hybrid Autoencoder-Multilayer Perceptron (AE-MLP) model

Research Questions

  1. Can a PCA-CNN or AE-MLP IDS achieve higher resource efficiency than a baseline 1D-CNN IDS, while maintaining comparable or superior performance in a resource-constrained environment?
  2. Can a quantised version of a PCA-CNN or AE-MLP IDS achieve higher resource efficiency than a quantised version of the 1D-CNN IDS baseline, while maintaining comparable or superior performance in a resource-constrained environment?
  3. Can PCA-CNN or AE-MLP IDS generalise effectively to previously unseen attack classes to unseen data more effectively than a baseline 1D-CNN?

Experiment Methodology

Benchmarking

The purpose of this experiment is to establish a performance and resource baseline by benchmarking the initial full precision (FP32) models against the baseline CNN. Benchmarking provides a fair comparison across the different model architectures and aids in answering the first research question of this study. Additionally, benchmarking determines whether the quantised versions of the models maintained comparable performance while improving resource efficiency.

Quantisation

This experiment investigates the impact of quantisation on the performance and resource efficiency of the FP32 baseline CNN, PCA-CNN and AE-MLP models, addressing the second research question of this study. This experiment evaluates the performance and resource consumption of the quantised variants (FP16, dynamic INT8 and full INT8) of the full precision (FP32) PCA-CNN and AE- MLP models. These quantised models are subsequently compared to the corresponding quantised versions of the baseline CNN.

Generalisation

This experiment evaluates the ability of the models and their quantised variants to generalise to previously unseen, zero-day attacks, addressing the third research question of this study. The objective of this experiment is to simulate realistic zero-day attack scenarios and assess whether the models can learn generalisable patterns of malicious behaviour rather than memorising known attack signatures.

Literature Review

This literature review examines developments, challenges, and breakthroughs in intrusion detection system (IDS) frameworks. It specifically investigates the feasibility of implementing lightweight AI-based IDSs in low-resource networks. The findings of this review provided the foundation for the design and development of the lightweight AI-IDS models presented in the final study. This review examines both traditional and AI-based IDS methods, as well as existing datasets and commonly used evaluation metrics. It then provides a detailed comparison of traditional IDSs with AI-based IDSs, and of machine learning IDSs with deep learning IDSs. The feasibility of implementing a lightweight DL or ML-DL hybrid IDS is also assessed, along with the implications of the reviewed literature for the LAIDS project. Finally, conclusions are drawn, and a path forward for the LAIDS project is outlined.

Final Paper

Abstract: Intrusion detection systems (IDSs) have become crucial to networks, particularly with the growth of IoT, rising cybercrime and increasingly sophisticated threats. Traditional intrusion detection systems struggle to scale effectively with these increases and to detect zero-day attacks. AI-based IDSs offer improved detection accuracy and adaptability, but are often too resource-intensive. The LAIDS (Lightweight AI-based Intrusion Detection System) project aims to address this gap by designing and evaluating lightweight AI-IDS models that balance strong detection capabilities with low resource usage. Three core model architectures are explored in this paper: a baseline CNN, a PCA-CNN and an AE-MLP, which are trained and evaluated with the CICIDS2017 dataset. These models, along with their quantised variants, are evaluated on their classification performance, resource efficiency and their ability to generalise to 'zero-day attacks'. The paper advances AI-based IDS solutions and demonstrates their suitability for resource-constrained environments.
