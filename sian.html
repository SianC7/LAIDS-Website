Contributions to the LAIDS Study

My contributions to the LAIDS Project involved developing, quantising, and evaluating three lightweight AI-based intrusion detection system models optimised for resource-constrained networks. The objective of this study was to investigate whether these models and their quantised variants could perform as well as or better than the baseline model and its quantised versions, while being more resource-efficient. Additionally, the study examined the models' ability to detect previously unseen attack classes in comparison to the baseline model.

The models developed in this study:

  • A baseline one-dimensional Convolutional Neural Network model (CNN), which was co-developed with my fellow LAIDS project members
  • A Principle Component Analysis enhanced Convolutional Neural Network model (PCA-CNN)
  • A hybrid Autoencoder-Multilayer Perceptron (AE-MLP) model

Research Questions

  1. Can a PCA-CNN or AE-MLP IDS achieve higher resource efficiency than a baseline 1D-CNN IDS, while maintaining comparable or superior performance in a resource-constrained environment?
  2. Can a quantised version of a PCA-CNN or AE-MLP IDS achieve higher resource efficiency than a quantised version of the 1D-CNN IDS baseline, while maintaining comparable or superior performance in a resource-constrained environment?
  3. Can a PCA-CNN or AE-MLP IDS generalise to unseen data more effectively than a baseline 1D-CNN?

Experiments

Benchmarking

The purpose of this experiment was to establish a performance and resource baseline by benchmarking the initial full precision (FP32) models against the baseline CNN.

Benchmarking provided a fair comparison across the different model architectures and helped in answering the first research question of this study.

Additionally, benchmarking determined whether the quantised versions of the models maintained comparable performance while improving resource efficiency.

Quantisation

This experiment investigated the impact of quantisation on the performance and resource efficiency of the FP32 baseline CNN, PCA-CNN, and AE-MLP models, addressing the second research question of this study.

It evaluated the performance and resource consumption of the quantised variants (FP16, dynamic INT8, and full INT8) of the full precision (FP32) PCA-CNN and AE-MLP models.These quantised models were subsequently compared to the corresponding quantised versions of the baseline CNN.

Generalisation

This experiment evaluated the ability of the models and their quantised variants to generalise to previously unseen, zero-day attacks, addressing the third research question of this study.

The objective of the experiment was to simulate realistic zero-day attack scenarios and assess whether the models could learn generalisable patterns of malicious behaviour rather than memorising known attack signatures.

Key Findings

Benchmarking

The baseline CNN achieved the highest accuracy (95.39%) and the lowest false positive rate (5.89%). However, the PCA-CNN provided a better recall rate (99.71%) along with faster CPU processing and inference times (0.048 ms and 0.0493 ms, respectively). Finally, the AE-MLP offered higher precision (90.53%) than the baseline model as well as the use of less memory (2.627 KB).

Quantisation

As the models underwent quantisation and were reduced in size, their overall performance decreased. The quantised PCA models, however, still continued to offer faster CPU processing and inference times compared to the baseline CNN models. Notably, the FP16 AE-MLP model achieved the lowest memory usage.

Generalisation

The PCA-CNN models generally showed slightly better detection performance than the baseline CNN models (approximately 2-3% higher). However, the semi-supervised AE-MLP achieved detection rates of around 60% for both DoS and Bot attacks, which were comparable to the PCA-CNN's DoS detection rate. This indicated that the AE-MLP could provide more consistent detection performance across different attack types. Overall, the models were able to detect DoS attacks more effectively than previously unseen Bot attacks.

Conclusion

The findings suggested that no model consistently outperformed the baseline in terms of detection performance and resource efficiency. However, each model demonstrated specific performance gains over the baseline. Notably, the dynamically INT8-quantised PCA-CNN showed the most promise for deployment as a lightweight intrusion detection system, achieving some of the lowest CPU processing times, inference latency, and false negative rates. Furthermore, it attained one of the highest detection rates for DoS samples when presented as zero-day attacks.

Overall, the findings indicated that developing a lightweight, effective IDS for resource-constrained networks may require combining feature reduction techniques, such as PCA, with semi-supervised learning approaches. This balance could help achieve strong detection accuracy, efficient resource usage, and improved detection of novel threats.

Documents

Literature Review

Abstract: This literature review examined developments, challenges, and breakthroughs in intrusion detection system (IDS) frameworks. It specifically investigated the feasibility of implementing lightweight AI-based IDSs in low-resource networks. The findings of this review provided the foundation for the design and development of the lightweight AI-IDS models presented in the final study. The review covered both traditional and AI-based IDS methods, as well as existing datasets and commonly used evaluation metrics. It then provided a detailed comparison of traditional IDSs with AI-based IDSs, and of machine learning IDSs with deep learning IDSs. The feasibility of implementing a lightweight DL or ML-DL hybrid IDS was also assessed, along with the implications of the reviewed literature for the LAIDS project. Finally, conclusions were drawn, and a path forward for the LAIDS project was outlined.


Open Literature Review

Final Paper

Abstract: Intrusion detection systems (IDSs) have become crucial to networks, particularly with the growth of IoT, rising cybercrime and increasingly sophisticated threats. Traditional intrusion detection systems struggle to scale effectively with these increases and to detect zero-day attacks. AI-based IDSs offer improved detection accuracy and adaptability, but are often too resource-intensive. The LAIDS (Lightweight AI-based Intrusion Detection System) project aims to address this gap by designing and evaluating lightweight AI-IDS models that balance strong detection capabilities with low resource usage. Three core model architectures are explored in this paper: a baseline CNN, a PCA-CNN and an AE-MLP, which are trained and evaluated with the CICIDS2017 dataset. These models, along with their quantised variants, are evaluated on their classification performance, resource efficiency and their ability to generalise to 'zero-day attacks'. The paper advances AI-based IDS solutions and demonstrates their suitability for resource-constrained environments.
